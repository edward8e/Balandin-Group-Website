Optothermal Raman (OTR) is a steady-state measurement technique, which directly determines thermal conductivity of a film. In this technique the Raman excitation laser acts as a heater to induce local heating in the film and micro Raman spectrometer is used as thermometer to determine this local temperature rise. The temperature dependency of Raman peak position enables one to read the local temperature of a material using Raman spectroscopy.

This method is especially useful for graphene-based materials owing to graphene’s distinctive Raman signature with clear G and 2D band. The OTR measurement procedure involved two steps: power-dependent Raman measurements and calibration measurements. In the power-dependent Raman measurements, the shift of Raman peak is recorded as a function of varying laser power. For these measurements the sample is suspended over heat sinks and therefore the Raman induced heating on the sample travels toward these heat sinks where the temperature remains as ambient. Figure X shows a schematic of this measurement for single layer Graphene suspended over gold heat sink.

In order to attribute the Raman peak shift recorded during the power-dependent Raman measurements, to the laser induced temperature rise, calibration measurements should be performed. In the calibration measurements, the Raman peak position is recorded as a function of sample temperature, which is controlled externally using cold-hot cell. The slope of this plot determines the temperature coefficient of Raman peak. In order to extract the thermal conductivity Fourier’s equation need to be solved for the specific sample geometry. Optothermal Raman Technique can be applied on both micro-scale and macro-scale films. Though its been originally introduced for single layer graphene, researchers have been using this technique for characterizing wide range of other 2D materials specially Transition Metal Dichalcogenides.
